Deal structuring (asset vs. stock) and step-up planning Standard, Illinois

Enrolled Agent (EA)

Deal structuring (asset vs. stock) and step-up planning are two levers that do the heavy lifting in middle-market transactions across Illinois, from Chicago to small towns like Standard. They shape tax outcomes, allocate risk, determine how quickly a buyer can realize value, and influence how cleanly a seller can exit. Getting them right requires aligning federal tax strategy with Illinois' state-specific rules and the practical realities of transferring a working business.


Asset purchase versus stock purchase at a glance



  • Asset purchase. The buyer acquires selected assets and assumes only the liabilities it agrees to assume. This is attractive to buyers who want a fresh start, a basis step-up in the acquired assets, and flexibility to leave behind unwanted liabilities. Sellers may face double taxation if the seller is a C corporation, and they must contend with consents to transfer contracts, permits, and licenses.

  • Stock purchase. The buyer acquires ownership of the entity and, by default, takes all assets and liabilities as they sit. Sellers typically prefer stock sales, especially if they are C corporations or have favorable shareholder-level rates, because they may achieve single-level taxation. Buyers lose the automatic basis step-up unless they implement a special election. Stock deals can be simpler on the operational side because contracts and permits usually stay in place, though change-of-control provisions still matter.


The economics of the step-up
At the federal level, asset deals (or stock deals treated as asset deals through elections) produce a step-up in tax basis to fair market value, creating future deductions for tangible assets via depreciation and for most intangibles, including goodwill, via 15-year amortization under section 197. This can materially increase after-tax cash flow for the buyer. Absent a step-up, the buyer inherits the seller's historic basis, often producing lower deductions and weaker early cash flow.

In Illinois, the state income tax base generally starts with federal taxable income, then applies state-specific adjustments. The key practical points:



  • Illinois conformity. Illinois generally conforms to federal depreciation and amortization rules but often decouples from bonus depreciation under section 168(k). That means a buyer might have to add back some federal bonus depreciation in the year of acquisition and recover it over time through state modifications. The step-up still creates value at the state level; the timing just differs.

  • Entity-level taxes. C corporations pay the Illinois corporate income tax plus the personal property replacement tax (together commonly approximating a combined mid-to-high single-digit percentage). S corporations and partnerships don't pay the corporate income tax but do owe the replacement tax at a lower rate. These differences affect modeling: a given step-up yields different Illinois cash tax benefits depending on entity type and apportionment.

  • Apportionment. Illinois uses single-sales-factor apportionment for most corporations. The incremental deductions from a step-up reduce the Illinois base in proportion to the Illinois sales footprint. A business with largely out-of-state sales will see a smaller Illinois benefit than one selling predominantly into Illinois.


Creating a step-up in stock deals
Buyers who want the operational simplicity of a stock deal but the economics of an asset deal have several tools:



  • Section 338(h)(10). Available for qualified stock purchases of an S corporation or a subsidiary in a consolidated group. Buyer and seller jointly elect to treat the transaction as a deemed asset sale for tax purposes. The buyer gets a step-up; the seller is taxed as if it sold assets. This typically pushes more tax to the seller than a plain stock sale, so price negotiation is crucial.

  • Section 336(e). Similar to 338(h)(10) in its effect but made by the seller in certain nonconsolidated situations.

  • Section 338(g). For C corporation targets where the above elections won't work. The buyer gets a step-up, but the target corporation recognizes gain, often creating a second layer of tax for the seller when proceeds are distributed. This can be expensive for sellers, again demanding careful gross-up and pricing.


In partnership and LLC contexts taxed as partnerships, a buyer can achieve a step-up by purchasing interests and having the partnership make a section 754 election, or by structuring as an asset acquisition or a “mixing bowl” transaction with care. Illinois generally follows the federal inside-basis adjustments with its own depreciation rules layered on top.


Illinois-specific asset deal pitfalls and advantages



  • Successor tax liability and bulk sale notice. Illinois imposes successor liability for certain unpaid taxes of the seller, including sales and withholding taxes, unless the buyer follows the bulk sale notification process with the Illinois Department of Revenue. Buyers typically file Form CBS-1 before closing to obtain a clearance or instructions to withhold from the purchase price. Skipping this step can leave a buyer on the hook for the seller's old liabilities.

  • Sales and use tax on asset transfers. Many transfers of business assets qualify for Illinois' occasional sale exemption, particularly when selling the business as a going concern, but the details matter. Items such as motor vehicles or titled equipment often require special transfer forms and may trigger use tax. Early scoping of the assets list avoids unpleasant surprises.

  • Licenses and permits. Some Illinois and local permits are nontransferable. Liquor, cannabis, gaming, and healthcare-related licenses, among others, require fresh approvals. If speed matters, a stock deal or a bridge management agreement with a later reorganization may be more practical.

  • Employment and unemployment insurance. Illinois successor rules can transfer unemployment experience to a buyer in an asset deal if continuity tests are met, affecting future contribution rates. Plan for this in your financial model.

  • Real property and local transfer taxes. If real estate is included, Illinois imposes state and county transfer taxes, and certain municipalities impose their own. Even in smaller communities like Standard, confirm with the county recorder and local authorities, budget for taxes and prorations, and line up title work early.


Practical negotiation dynamics



  • Buyer preferences. Buyers typically prefer asset deals for the liability shield and the step-up. If the seller resists, buyers may pay more for a stock deal with a 338(h)(10) or 336(e) election to reclaim economics. Failing that, the price often moves to reflect the lost tax shield.

  • Seller preferences. Sellers prefer stock sales-especially C corporations-to avoid double tax and to keep life simple. Owners of S corporations with low shareholder bases may be more open to a 338(h)(10) if the price is right.

  • Purchase price allocation. In asset deals and deemed asset deals, the parties must agree on an allocation across asset classes under section 1060. Allocations to fixed assets and amortizable intangibles accelerate buyer deductions but can increase seller tax on depreciation recapture and ordinary income. In Illinois, because of bonus depreciation decoupling, the time profile of state benefits might differ from the federal profile, which can influence how much a buyer is willing to pay for a step-up.


Timing, elections, and compliance



  • Build the step-up into LOI terms. Spell out whether the deal will be an asset sale, a stock sale with a 338(h)(10) or 336(e) election, or a partnership 754 election. Head off disputes by agreeing on a purchase price allocation framework early.

  • Calendar the filings. Federal step-up elections are deadline-driven. The Illinois bulk sale notice should be filed before closing to secure tax clearance. Missing a deadline can permanently forfeit benefits or create successor liability.

  • Model federal and Illinois together. The best structure on a federal basis might be slightly less optimal for Illinois because of bonus depreciation addbacks or apportionment. A combined model prevents whipsaw outcomes.


When a step-up isn't worth it
Sometimes the premium needed to persuade a seller to accept a deemed asset sale outweighs the buyer's tax benefit. Examples include targets with net operating losses that offset much of the gain, or businesses with short-lived assets where the remaining basis difference is small. In Illinois, where the timing of deductions can be slower, the present value of the state benefit may not justify a steep gross-up. In those cases, a plain stock deal with a price concession and tight indemnities may be the better call.

Smaller Illinois communities often feature family-owned S corporations or LLCs where relationships with employees, customers, and local officials matter as much as tax math.

Bottom line



  • If you want clean assets and a basis step-up, lean asset deal or a stock deal with a 338(h)(10) or 336(e) election.

  • If seller tax friction is high, price and structure must share the load; sometimes the right answer is a stock deal with robust protections.

  • In Illinois, always layer in bulk sale compliance, bonus depreciation decoupling, apportionment, and local transfer rules.

Frequently Asked Questions

In Standard, Illinois, is an asset purchase or a stock purchase typically better from a tax and risk standpoint?
For buyers, asset deals usually win: you get a federal and Illinois basis step-up (IRC 1060) including 15-year goodwill amortization and can leave behind unknown liabilities; but you must manage possible taxes/fees on specific assets and more transfer steps. For sellers, stock deals often win: simpler closing and potentially one level of tax (e.g., S-corp/individual), but no buyer step-up unless a special election. Always model both after Illinois rates (e.g., ~9.5% for C-corps including replacement tax; 1.5% replacement tax for S-corps/partnerships) and apportionment.
How can we obtain a basis step-up if we want to do a stock deal?
Use elections that treat stock as assets: 338(h)(10) for S-corps or members of a consolidated C-corp group; 336(e) in certain nonconsolidated C- or S-corp sales; 338(g) for standalone C-corps (seller pays inside corporate tax). For LLCs/partnerships, make a 754 election to create a 743(b) inside basis step-up for the buyer. Illinois generally conforms, so the step-up flows to Illinois.
