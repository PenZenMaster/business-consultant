Start with the bedrock choice: cash versus accrual. Under the federal small business gross receipts test, many La Salle businesses qualify for the cash method, which recognizes income when received and expenses when paid. For service firms, restaurants, and small distributors, this can smooth cash flow and defer income in leaner years. If you carry significant inventory, you still might qualify as a “small business taxpayer,” which opens a door to simplified inventory accounting under section 471(c). Instead of formal inventories, you can treat items as non-incidental materials and supplies and deduct them when used or consumed, aligned with how your books capture cost of goods. This can reduce year-end surprises and compliance cost.


Manufacturers and distributors with rising input costs should evaluate inventory flow assumptions. FIFO mirrors physical flow for many operations, but it can accelerate taxable income when prices are climbing. LIFO, properly elected and maintained, can better match current costs to current sales and reduce taxable income in inflationary periods. The election is made with Form 970 and needs discipline to maintain, but the long-run tax and cash benefits can be meaningful for a plant on North 30th Road holding months of resin, steel, or chemicals.


Contractors should examine long-term contract methods. Small contractors may be able to use the completed contract method for eligible jobs, deferring income until the job is done. Others will use percentage-of-completion, but the choice of cost-to-cost measures, handling of change orders, and the recurring item exception can still fine-tune timing. For home construction contracts, special rules may allow more deferral. The key is to align what your jobs look like in practice with a method the IRS permits and that your bonding company understands.


Depreciation and capitalization elections are where many La Salle businesses can unlock cash flow. Section 179 expensing allows you to expense qualifying equipment up to annual limits, and bonus depreciation can write off much of the remainder.

On the state side, Illinois offers a pass-through entity (PTE) tax election that serves as a workaround to the federal SALT cap; the entity pays Illinois tax at the entity level and owners receive a credit. This can be attractive for S corporations and partnerships whose owners would otherwise lose the deduction at the personal level. But the PTE election changes quarterly estimates, partner/shareholder cash needs, and return mechanics. Decide early, model the cash impact, and make sure your bank covenants and buy‑sell agreements expect the shift.


Entity type and state-level frictions also influence optimization.
