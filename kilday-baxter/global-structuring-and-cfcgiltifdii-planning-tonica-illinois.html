Global structuring and CFC/GILTI/FDII planning Tonica, Illinois

LaSalle County, Illinois

Standing in Tonica, Illinois, it can be tempting to think of “global structuring” as something reserved for Fortune 100 boardrooms. In reality, the forces shaping cross‑border tax rules reach all the way to small and midsize companies along the I‑39 corridor-manufacturers that ship replacement parts to Canada, ag‑tech businesses testing new markets in South America, local founders selling software subscriptions to customers in Europe, or family groups with a sales office in Mexico. For these businesses, getting global structuring and CFC/GILTI/FDII planning right can be the difference between a predictable tax burden that supports growth and a patchwork of costly surprises.


The basic building blocks are straightforward once you unpack the jargon. A CFC, or Controlled Foreign Corporation, is a non‑U.S. company more than 50 percent owned by U.S. shareholders that each hold at least 10 percent. GILTI, Global Intangible Low‑Taxed Income, is a U.S. anti‑deferral regime that can pull a CFC's earnings into the U.S. tax base each year, even if you do not repatriate cash. FDII, Foreign‑Derived Intangible Income, is the mirror image for certain U.S. exporters: a deduction that can lower the effective U.S. rate on income from serving foreign markets. These rules were designed for large multinationals, but they apply mechanically to any U.S. owner that meets the thresholds, including closely held businesses in places like Tonica.


Why this matters locally is easy to illustrate. Imagine a Tonica manufacturer that forms a wholly owned subsidiary in Poland to assemble components closer to European customers. Without planning, the Polish profits might be swept into U.S. income as GILTI, taxed at the U.S. level with limited foreign tax credit relief. At the same time, if the U.S. parent holds the key contracts with overseas customers, some of that export income might qualify for FDII, trimming the federal rate on profits tied to foreign sales.

Getting entity choices, intercompany pricing, and cash flows aligned can turn a rough equation into one that supports margins and reinvestment.


Several practical levers tend to have the biggest impact.


First, entity choice and elections. For C corporations, GILTI and FDII interplay through the section 250 deduction (subject to scheduled changes under current law). C corporations also get access to a partial foreign tax credit against GILTI, though it sits in its own basket and is limited to 80 percent with no carryforward.

Pass‑through owners face a different landscape: they do not get the same section 250 relief automatically, and their ability to use foreign tax credits is more constrained. Early in the planning cycle, decide whether a U.S. C corporation holding company is the right platform for your cross‑border expansion, or whether a pass‑through structure with careful ownership and compensation design better fits your goals. A simple “check‑the‑box” election on a foreign subsidiary can also change whether income is pooled or isolated for GILTI and foreign tax credit computations.


Second, the GILTI base itself. GILTI is calculated on tested income across your CFCs after a routine return on tangible assets (QBAI). High‑taxed CFC income may be eligible for the GILTI high‑tax exclusion if the local effective rate clears the regulatory threshold and you make a timely election.

    In practice, that means modeling not just the foreign statutory rate but also incentives, withholding taxes, and expense allocations that affect the effective rate. Sometimes the right answer is to group CFCs with similar profiles; sometimes it is better to separate profitable and loss‑making entities to avoid cross‑contamination in the tested income pool. Intercompany royalties, interest, and service fees need to be priced at arm's length and structured with withholding and deductibility in mind, but they also influence where income lands for GILTI, Subpart F, and local country purposes.


    Third, foreign tax credits and expense allocation. The math here is technical, but the practical takeaway for a Tonica business is simple: small changes in how you book interest, stewardship costs, and research expenses can swing your ability to credit foreign taxes in the GILTI or general baskets. The section 78 gross‑up, the 861 allocation rules, and capitalization requirements all feed the credit limit. You do not need to become a specialist; you do need a model that ties your chart of accounts to the required forms so there are no surprises at filing time.


    Fourth, the FDII opportunity. FDII can reward a U.S. C corporation that earns income from serving non‑U.S. customers with property or services. It is not just for owners of patents. If your Tonica plant ships finished goods abroad, if your engineers design prototypes for a German customer, or if your support team serves Canadian subscribers, some portion of that profit may qualify. The key is documentation: proving foreign use or consumption, bifurcating domestic and foreign revenue streams, and supporting transfer pricing. FDII planning sits alongside, not in place of, time‑tested export incentives. For certain manufacturers, an IC‑DISC can complement or, in some cases, be outperformed by FDII; running the numbers based on margins and customer mix will point the way.


    Fifth, supply chain and substance. GILTI and FDII are tax levers, but they only work within a business that has real substance and sensible transfer pricing. If your foreign subsidiary is performing meaningful functions, taking risks, and owning assets, it must earn an arm's‑length return, and your documentation should say so. Conversely, if the engine of value creation is in LaSalle County-your engineers, tooling, and quality control-then keeping key intangibles and decision‑making in the U.S. can simplify life, support FDII benefits, and limit volatility abroad.


    Compliance should not be an afterthought. Owning or controlling foreign entities brings with it forms and penalties you do not want to discover too late. Most U.S. owners of CFCs will file Forms 5471 and 8992 for GILTI, 8993 for the section 250 deduction, and 1118 or 1116 for foreign tax credits. Interests in foreign funds or startups may trigger PFIC reporting on Form 8621. If you operate through disregarded branches, the information reporting is different but no less important. Build the data pipeline early: local statutory accounts, trial balances on a U.S. GAAP schedule, and a calendar that tracks tax holidays, withholding tax due dates, and intercompany true‑ups.


    There are also wider developments to keep in view. The global minimum tax (Pillar Two) adopted by many countries imposes top‑up taxes to 15 percent for large groups. While most Tonica businesses are below the 750‑million‑euro threshold, suppliers to larger groups may feel indirect pressure to align. The U.S. has not adopted Pillar Two, and the interaction with GILTI and foreign credits continues to evolve. Domestically, the book minimum tax applies only to very large corporations, but the scheduled reductions to the section 250 deduction after 2025 would, if left unchanged, raise effective rates on both GILTI and FDII. Planning should be resilient to these shifts rather than relying on a single number.


    State taxes add another layer. States differ widely in how they treat GILTI, foreign dividends, and the section 250 deduction, and those rules change. Illinois generally starts with federal taxable income and then makes adjustments; its treatment of foreign‑source dividends, GILTI inclusions, and related deductions is distinct and has been updated more than once in recent years. Because state conformity can swing the overall benefit of a federal strategy, model Illinois and any other filing states alongside your federal plan before you lock in structure.


    The human side matters, too. Many cross‑border ventures in places like Tonica are family‑owned and run lean. Decisions about whether to hold the foreign subsidiary at the operating company or a new holding company, how to compensate key people abroad, how to move cash back for new equipment, and how to plan for succession all intersect with the tax picture. A structure that looks brilliant on a whiteboard but strains your bank covenants, complicates audits, or ties up cash in trapped earnings is not a win. Align the tax plan with banking, insurance, and operational realities.


    If you are just getting started, a sensible roadmap looks like this: map your current and projected foreign touchpoints; choose the U.S. platform (C corp or pass‑through) with eyes open to GILTI, FDII, and credits; design the foreign entity footprint with substance and high‑tax exclusion opportunities in mind; set transfer pricing policies that reflect where work is actually done; and build a light but reliable compliance engine. From Tonica, you may not need a big‑city tax department, but you do need a playbook, a calendar, and a named advisor you can call before you sign a contract overseas.


    Global structuring and CFC/GILTI/FDII planning is not about chasing loopholes. It is about using the rules as written to support the real economics of your business, minimize friction, and keep your attention on customers. Done well, it helps a company from a small Illinois village compete confidently on a big stage. And as always, because facts and laws change, pair this general guidance with tailored advice from a tax professional who understands both international rules and the practicalities of operating from Tonica, Illinois.
