State, Local & Indirect Taxes Standard, Illinois

General Partnerships

State, local, and indirect taxes in Illinois form an intricate web that touches almost every kind of transaction a business or household might make. When people talk about a “standard” approach to Illinois SALT-state and local taxes-they usually mean a practical framework for understanding the main taxes, how they interact, and the everyday decisions that keep an organization compliant without overpaying. Illinois layers a straightforward state income tax system on top of complex retail and local taxes, with Chicago in particular adding a dense array of levies on specific activities. The result is a landscape where the basics are simple, but the details matter a great deal.


Start with income taxes. Illinois is known for its flat individual income tax rate, which makes paycheck withholding and personal planning feel predictable.

For businesses, the corporate income tax is paired with a personal property replacement tax, a legacy levy that replaced local personal property taxes decades ago. For C corporations, the combination produces an effective state-level burden higher than the nominal corporate rate alone. Pass-through entities-partnerships and S corporations-typically flow income to owners, but they still face a replacement tax at the entity level, and Illinois now allows an elective pass-through entity tax designed to help owners navigate the federal cap on state and local tax deductions. The tax base rules are also important. Illinois relies on single-sales-factor apportionment for most businesses and generally looks to where the customer receives the benefit when sourcing service revenue, a market-based approach that can shift tax liability meaningfully for service, software, and digital commerce companies.


If income taxes are the backbone, Illinois's indirect taxes are the circulatory system, constantly in motion as goods and services change hands. What many call “sales tax” in Illinois is, technically, the Retailers' Occupation Tax-an occupation tax on sellers-paired with a complementary use tax that applies to buyers who use items in Illinois without tax having been collected. The state base rate is well-known, but the action is local: counties and municipalities often stack home rule and special district taxes on top of the state rate, pushing combined retail rates above 10 percent in some areas. Chicago is the most visible example, though high combined rates appear throughout the state. The sourcing rules-where a transaction is taxed-are nuanced. In-state retailers look to where selling activities occur, while remote retailers and marketplace facilitators generally collect at the destination rate under Illinois's “Leveling the Playing Field” reforms. The intent was to reduce the competitive edge once enjoyed by out-of-state sellers and to ensure local governments receive their share when their residents buy from afar.


Illinois largely taxes tangible goods, not services, but there are many exceptions and specialized rules. The Service Occupation Tax and Service Use Tax apply when a service provider transfers tangible personal property incidental to a service-think repair parts or fabricated items. Manufacturing and agriculture enjoy important exemptions, including a broad manufacturing machinery and equipment exemption that Illinois expanded in recent years to cover more production-related items. Retailers document exemptions with certificates and must maintain strong procedures to defend those positions under audit.

For companies that buy from vendors who do not collect Illinois tax, use tax accrual-quietly and consistently posting the correct tax on purchases-is a hallmark of a well-run tax function.


Local taxes in Illinois go well beyond add-ons to retail rates. Chicago, as a home rule city, imposes a suite of targeted excise taxes that can surprise newcomers. The Personal Property Lease Transaction Tax reaches many cloud and software-as-a-service offerings, treating remote computing and certain digital products as taxable leases of property. The Amusement Tax applies to entertainment and streaming services. There are also hotel, parking, restaurant, and specialized utility and telecommunications levies. Because these taxes rely on specific definitions and sourcing concepts-what is “use” of a cloud service, where is an “amusement” enjoyed-contract language, IP addresses, billing addresses, and actual usage patterns can all influence taxability. Vendors often collect these taxes, but liability can shift if contracts are structured in certain ways or if a taxpayer self-provisions services across multiple locations.


Property tax is another distinctive feature of the Illinois landscape. Administered primarily at the county level, property taxes fund schools and local services and are among the highest in the nation on a rate basis. Assessments rely on equalized values, and Cook County uses a classification system that assigns different assessment levels to residential, commercial, and industrial property. This dynamic, combined with frequent reassessments and appeal opportunities, makes property tax planning a recurring exercise rather than a once-and-done event. Many businesses engage in annual review cycles, marshaling valuation evidence and tracking local board and tribunal calendars to ensure they are not carrying an unnecessarily high assessment.


Industry-specific excise taxes round out the indirect tax picture. Illinois imposes state taxes on motor fuel, cigarettes and other tobacco products, and alcoholic beverages. The state's legal cannabis market features a tiered excise structure based on product type and potency, layered on top of local options. Telecommunications is governed by a state excise tax and municipal telecom taxes, with infrastructure fees added in some jurisdictions. Transportation and hospitality sectors encounter their own set of occupancy and ride-related taxes, especially in larger cities. While each of these is narrow, together they can be consequential for businesses with diverse revenue lines or complex billing models.


Illinois pairs its taxes with a portfolio of incentives meant to attract investment and jobs. The Economic Development for a Growing Economy (EDGE) credit can offset income tax for qualifying projects that meet job creation and capital thresholds. Enterprise zones and high-impact business designations provide sales tax exemptions on building materials and utilities, and sometimes property tax abatements, negotiated locally. The state's data center exemption, designed to compete in a national arms race for large-scale facilities, offers robust relief to projects that meet investment and sustainability commitments. Manufacturing exemptions, research and development credits, and training grants can further reduce cost structures for firms that plan ahead and track qualified spending.


Administratively, Illinois is fairly modern. Businesses register and file through MyTax Illinois, and most returns and payments are electronic. Filing frequencies vary with volume, and retailers may receive a small discount for timely collection and remittance, subject to caps. Marketplace facilitator rules centralize collection for third-party sales, but sellers still need to understand whether they remain liable for direct or use taxes on their own purchases and for sales outside the facilitator model. Nexus-the obligation to register and collect-can arise from physical presence or economic thresholds based on sales volume, and these rules continue to evolve for remote commerce. Illinois runs an active audit program, and the Department of Revenue offers voluntary disclosure pathways for taxpayers that need to come into compliance proactively.


What does a “standard” approach to Illinois state, local, and indirect taxes look like in practice? It starts with mapping your footprint: where you sell, where you deliver, where your people and property sit, and how your contracts describe your products and services. Next comes a taxability matrix for goods, services, and digital offerings, with special attention to Chicago and other home rule jurisdictions. Layer in exemption documentation and use tax accrual controls on the purchasing side. For income and replacement taxes, align apportionment with revenue recognition and customer location data, and revisit the elective pass-through entity tax if you operate through partnerships or S corporations.

For property tax, calendar the assessment cycle and build a playbook for appeals. Finally, set up a monitoring routine: Illinois changes rules periodically, local rates update often, and new technologies can pull you into taxes that did not exist when your original systems were built.


Illinois can be demanding, but it rewards diligence. Companies that take the time to understand the state's mix of broad-based and targeted taxes, and that invest in clean data and clear contracts, tend to avoid unpleasant surprises. And because local add-ons and special taxes can be material, especially in Chicago, a little upfront analysis can return many times its cost. As always, because tax rules change and facts matter, it is wise to confirm current rates and requirements with official guidance or a qualified advisor before making decisions.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do we need to collect Illinois sales tax, and when do we have nexus?
You must collect if you have physical presence in Illinois (e.g., employees, inventory, office, installers) or economic nexus—generally over $100,000 in Illinois sales in the prior 12 months. Remote sellers and marketplace facilitators are covered by Illinois’ Leveling the Playing Field rules; facilitators must collect on marketplace sales. If you sell both on and off marketplaces, register and collect on your direct sales.
What rate applies and how do we source the sale?
Illinois uses combined state and local Retailers’ Occupation/Use Taxes that vary by delivery location. Remote sellers and marketplace facilitators collect at destination (ship-to) rates; over-the-counter sales use the store’s origin rate. Use the Illinois rate lookup or certified software and calculate by precise ship-to address.
Which of our products/services are taxable or exempt?
Tangible personal property is generally taxable; most services are not. Common exemptions: resale (Form CRT-61) and qualified manufacturing machinery and equipment/production-related property (Form ST-587). Separately stated delivery by a common carrier may be non-taxable; handling is taxable. Note: Chicago local taxes (e.g., Personal Property Lease Transaction Tax and Amusement Tax) can apply to SaaS, cloud access, and streaming even when state sales tax does not.
How do we register, file, and manage consumer use tax?
Obtain an Illinois Business Tax (IBT) number via MyTax Illinois and add a sales/use tax account. File Form ST-1 monthly, quarterly, or annually based on volume; local add-ons are included on the same return. Accrue and report consumer use tax on untaxed purchases in your ST-1 and retain exemption certificates and location records for audit support.
We have prior Illinois exposure—what are our options?
Consider Illinois’ voluntary disclosure program to limit lookback and reduce penalties; Chicago offers separate VDAs for its local taxes. Quantify exposure by period and tax type (state ROT/use, Chicago lease/amusement), begin compliant collection prospectively, then pursue VDA before registering or being contacted by the state/city.
