Deal structuring (asset vs. stock) and step-up planning Utica, Illinois

Peru, IL 61354

Deal structuring in a small-market setting like Utica, Illinois isn't just a choice between asset and stock-it's a careful balance of tax outcomes, risk management, local permits, financing realities, and post-closing operations in a community where reputation, workforce, and relationships matter. Whether you're buying a riverside restaurant serving Starved Rock visitors, a light industrial shop, or a family-owned service company, the structure you choose will shape your cash flows and headaches for years.


Start with the business realities. SBA-backed financing, which is common in transactions under roughly $5–10 million in places like Utica, often nudges buyers toward asset purchases because lenders prefer the cleaner liability profile and tangible collateral. In hospitality and retail, local licenses are easier to reset under an asset deal than to inherit and amend under a stock deal. For closely held businesses with decades of accumulated equipment, goodwill, and customer lists, the tax basis is often low-so the buyer's ability to step up that basis can be a major value driver.


Asset sale: clean slate and a step-up you can use



  • Tax basis step-up.

    The defining advantage for buyers is a fresh tax basis in the assets they acquire. Equipment and furniture get depreciated faster than real estate; intangible assets like customer relationships and goodwill amortize over 15 years. That future tax shield can materially reduce the effective purchase price. The buyer and seller negotiate a purchase price allocation that follows federal rules for classes of assets; getting this right directly affects both parties' taxes.

  • Pick your assets and limit liabilities. You normally choose which assets and contracts you want and leave most old liabilities behind. In practice, some liabilities can still follow the business (taxes, environmental, employment, or vendor claims attached to specific assets), so diligence and indemnities still matter.

  • Practical Illinois points. Illinois has a “bulk sales” regime administered by the Department of Revenue. Filing the Notice of Sale, Purchase, or Transfer of Business Assets (Form CBS‑1) before closing is critical; it helps you avoid being tagged with the seller's unpaid sales, use, or withholding taxes and gives the state time to identify liabilities. In asset deals, certain items may be taxable in Illinois: inventory sales are usually taxable at retail, while the occasional sale of business equipment can be exempt-facts and documentation drive the outcome. Work with a sales tax specialist early.

  • Licenses and permits. In an Utica hospitality purchase, you'll likely need new applications for village business and liquor licenses, food service permits, gaming, and state sales tax registration. Don't assume transferability. Build the licensing timeline into closing conditions and escrows.

  • Real estate. If real property is included, expect deed transfer taxes at the state and county level and a likely reassessment for property tax purposes. LaSalle County assessments and equalization factors can shift your operating costs; negotiate tax proration and post-closing appeal rights in the purchase agreement. For river-adjacent sites, budget for floodplain due diligence and, if relevant, environmental reviews.


Stock sale: continuity and simplicity-if you can price the risk



  • Continuity. A stock deal keeps contracts, permits, and employees in place with fewer disruptions-a plus for businesses where customer relationships or specialized licenses are fragile. In very small companies, this may be less compelling if key permits don't readily transfer anyway.

  • Tax trade-offs. Buyers do not get an inside basis step-up by default, so there's no new depreciation on the company's existing assets. That typically means a lower after-tax return unless the price adjusts down. Sellers often prefer stock sales-especially C‑corporation owners-because stock sales can avoid entity-level tax on an asset gain and simplify their own tax reporting.

  • Liability inheritance. You buy the company warts and all. This calls for deeper diligence, robust representations and warranties, escrows, and sometimes rep-and-warranty insurance. In Illinois, pay special attention to employment practices, environmental history (for industrial or fuel-related businesses), sales and withholding tax compliance, and any IDES unemployment insurance matters that can carry over.


Step-up planning when a stock deal makes sense
Sometimes the operational or licensing logic favors a stock purchase, but the buyer still wants the tax step-up. Three federal tools are common:



  • Section 338(h)(10) election. Available when buying stock of an S corporation or a subsidiary in a consolidated C‑corp group. It treats the deal as if the target sold its assets and then liquidated, creating a buyer step-up but often triggering seller-level tax similar to an asset sale. This can be a win-win if price and allocation are negotiated with eyes open.

  • Section 336(e) election. Similar to 338(h)(10) in S‑corp contexts and certain C‑corp shareholder sales; less commonly used but worth evaluating.

  • Partnerships/LLCs taxed as partnerships. A 754 election can step up the basis of the partnership's assets for the buyer when interests change hands. This is often the cleanest route for local businesses that were formed as LLCs years ago.


Illinois-specific tax and compliance angles that move the needle



  • Illinois income and replacement taxes. Illinois imposes entity-level taxes in addition to individual or corporate income taxes. In S‑corp and partnership settings, entity-level replacement tax can apply to gains in asset sales. In C‑corp asset deals, an entity-level tax hits first, followed by shareholder tax on distributions-double taxation that often pushes sellers to seek stock treatment or higher prices. Run side-by-side models; seemingly small rate differences change deal economics.

  • Depreciation conformity. Illinois has historically decoupled from certain federal accelerated depreciation (like bonus depreciation) in some years. That can affect the timing of your state tax benefits from a step-up. Confirm current-year rules with your CPA.

  • Sales and use tax cleanup. Beyond filing the CBS‑1, scrub historical sales tax filing patterns. Restaurants and retailers in tourist-driven Utica can have seasonal spikes, mixed rates, and delivery/pickup wrinkles that create exposure. Consider a targeted sales tax review during diligence and use escrow to address findings.

  • Employment and payroll. If you're acquiring a business with a steady workforce, map out payroll tax filings, wage/hour compliance, and whether any experience rating transfers could occur. In a stock deal, unemployment insurance history carries forward; in asset deals, partial transfers can still occur in certain continuity scenarios.

  • Local taxes and fees. Lodging or amusement taxes can apply to hospitality assets near Starved Rock. Confirm registration requirements with the Village of North Utica and LaSalle County well before closing.


Negotiation points that protect value



  • Purchase price allocation. In asset and deemed-asset deals, agree early on the allocation across equipment, inventory, real property, and intangibles. The buyer wants more in short-lived assets; the seller may prefer allocations that minimize ordinary income (for example, recapture on equipment or certain intangibles).

  • Working capital and seasonality. Utica's tourism seasonality can swing inventory, gift card liabilities, and cash balances. Tailor the working capital peg to the unique cadence of a riverside restaurant or outfitter rather than a flat average.

  • Indemnities, escrows, and RWI. Even in asset deals, build specific indemnities for pre-closing taxes, wage claims, gift cards, and any environmental or food safety matters. Consider rep-and-warranty insurance in mid-market transactions to bridge risk tolerance.

  • Permits as closing conditions. Make license approvals, landlord consents, and key vendor assignments explicit closing conditions. For liquor in particular, plan the timing with the village clerk and state authorities; interim management agreements may be needed to avoid downtime.

  • Real estate diligence. Order a Phase I environmental assessment where fuel, solvents, or historic industrial uses are present. Review zoning, special use permits, and floodplain maps; Utica's proximity to the Illinois River makes this practical, not theoretical.

Process and team



  • Build a modeling loop. Compare after-tax outcomes under asset versus stock (and deemed-asset) structures for both sides. The “better” structure often depends on which side values the tax attributes more-and price can bridge the gap.

  • Engage local know-how. A CPA and attorney experienced with Illinois IDOR bulk sales procedures, local licensing, and LaSalle County property tax practices will save time and surprises. Call the Village of North Utica early about license timing.

  • Paper it well. Use a purchase agreement that fits the structure you choose-asset or stock-with clear disclosure schedules, tax covenants, and post-closing cooperation clauses (especially for IRS/IDOR forms, purchase price allocation filings, and 338/336/754 elections).

Bottom line
In Utica, the practical default for many small business transfers is an asset deal backed by SBA financing and anchored by a buyer's tax step-up and cleaner liability profile. But the “right” answer is the one that maximizes after-tax value while preserving operations and community relationships. If licensing continuity, key contracts, or corporate history make a stock deal attractive, pair it with a 338(h)(10), 336(e), or 754 plan to recapture much of the step-up benefit. Whatever route you take, treat Illinois-specific tax compliance-especially the bulk sales notice-as part of the closing checklist, not an afterthought. And get local advisors involved early; in a market like Utica, details and timing make all the difference.

Frequently Asked Questions

For a buyer and seller in Illinois, what are the key tax trade-offs between an asset purchase and a stock purchase?
Asset purchase: buyer gets a basis step-up in assets (including 15-year amortization of Section 197 intangibles) and can cost-segregate; may face sales/use tax on tangible assets unless an exemption (e.g., occasional/bulk sale) applies; seller may incur depreciation recapture and, for C corporations, potential double tax. Stock purchase: generally simpler and often better for sellers (capital gain, avoids entity-level tax for C corps); buyer inherits liabilities and usually gets no basis step-up unless a special election is made.
How can we get a step-up in basis if we prefer a stock deal structure?
Use an IRC 338(h)(10) election (for S corps and certain subsidiaries when buyer is a corporation) or an IRC 336(e) election (S corp stock sales with seller/S corp consent); for partnerships/LLCs taxed as partnerships, use a Section 754 election; or use pre-/post-closing entity “check-the-box” or merger into a disregarded entity. Illinois generally follows the federal basis and depreciation results.
What Illinois- and Utica-specific issues should we plan for in closing?
Obtain Illinois Department of Revenue successor-liability (bulk sale) clearance to avoid liability for the seller’s unpaid taxes; document any sales/use tax exemptions on bulk asset transfers; address state/county real estate transfer taxes and property tax proration on any real property; handle title/registration for vehicles/equipment; consider Illinois Personal Property Replacement Tax and post-closing apportionment; and update Illinois/Utica registrations, withholding, unemployment accounts, and local licenses.
