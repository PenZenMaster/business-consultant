Global structuring and CFC/GILTI/FDII planning Peru, Illinois

Startup Advisory Services

Global structuring is no longer a big‑city or big‑tech concept. It's a practical necessity for midwestern manufacturers, distributors, and service firms-right down to places like Peru, Illinois-whose supply chains, customers, and teams now span borders. Whether you're sourcing components from Asia, contracting with a software team in Latin America, or opening a sales office in Europe, U.S. international tax rules will shape your after‑tax returns. Three acronyms sit at the center of that landscape: CFC, GILTI, and FDII.


At its core, global structuring means deciding where to place people, assets, risks, and intellectual property so that the business can operate efficiently and compliantly while keeping taxes predictable. For U.S. owners, the Controlled Foreign Corporation (CFC) regime and the GILTI rules pull certain foreign earnings into current U.S. taxation, while FDII can reward serving foreign markets from the United States. Getting these pieces to work together is the art.


CFCs, GILTI, and what they actually do



  • A CFC is a non‑U.S. company more than 50% owned (by vote or value) by U.S. shareholders. CFC status triggers U.S. anti‑deferral rules. Historically that meant Subpart F income-certain passive, mobile, and related‑party income-was taxed currently to U.S. owners.

  • GILTI (Global Intangible Low‑Taxed Income) adds a broader net. In simple terms, most active foreign profits of a CFC above a routine return on tangible assets are taxed annually to U.S. shareholders. The policy aim is to discourage shifting high‑margin profits to low‑tax jurisdictions.

  • FDII (Foreign‑Derived Intangible Income) is the domestic counterweight. If a U.S. C corporation earns profit from sales or services to customers outside the United States, it may get a deduction that lowers the effective rate on that export‑like income, provided specific documentation and “foreign use” tests are met.


For a business in Peru, Illinois, these rules matter even if the only foreign footprint is a contract manufacturer abroad or a small subsidiary selling into Canada or Latin America. The way you structure that footprint will influence how much income is taxed in the United States each year, how much credit you get for foreign taxes, and whether you can benefit from FDII.


Planning levers that actually move the needle
1) Choice of entity and elections



  • Subsidiary versus branch: A foreign branch's profits and losses flow through immediately and can create complex foreign tax credit (FTC) interactions. A subsidiary (CFC) introduces GILTI and Subpart F, but can allow for deferral in some cases and clearer FTC management.

  • Check‑the‑box: Electing to treat certain foreign entities as disregarded or as corporations can simplify intercompany flows, reduce Subpart F risk, or consolidate profits and losses for GILTI calculations. The right answer depends on local law and treaty availability.

3) Using FDII the right way



  • FDII only benefits C corporations. If Peru, Illinois owners hold the business through an S corporation or partnership, they won't get FDII directly. In some cases, a C‑corp blocker or an entity restructuring may be worth modeling.

  • Documentation of foreign use is not optional. To claim FDII, you must substantiate that goods are resold or used outside the United States, or that services are provided to a foreign person or with foreign benefit. Contracts, shipping records, customer attestations, and system flags matter.

  • Operational balance: FDII rewards high margin on foreign sales after a routine return on U.S. tangible assets. Investments in automation that raise margins can improve FDII, but shifting too much tangible asset base can also reduce the benefit. It's a calibration exercise, not a guessing game.


4) Intellectual property and transfer pricing



  • Where your IP “lives” and who performs DEMPE functions (development, enhancement, maintenance, protection, exploitation) drive both tax and operational realities. Cost‑sharing, licensing, or keeping IP onshore each carry different GILTI/FDII tradeoffs and 367(d) export implications.

  • Intercompany pricing must be defendable. Routine distributor or contract manufacturer models can reduce foreign residual profit that would otherwise become GILTI, but only if functions, risks, and documentation match the story.


5) Owners matter: corporate versus individual



  • C corporations enjoy the Section 250 deduction for GILTI and FDII, often cutting the effective U.S. rate significantly. Individuals and pass‑through owners generally do not, unless an individual makes a Section 962 election to be taxed like a corporation on GILTI and claim indirect FTCs. That election can help, but it adds complexity and sometimes state tax friction.

  • The Section 250 percentages for GILTI and FDII are scheduled to decline after 2025 under current law, which would raise effective rates. Timing decisions on investments, IP alignment, and entity choice should consider that horizon.


State and local angles for an Illinois‑based business



  • Illinois conformity to federal international provisions has evolved. The state generally provides a subtraction or dividend‑received deduction treatment for a significant portion of foreign dividends, and it has had special rules for GILTI and FDII. Because rules and guidance change, model your Illinois impact rather than assume federal outcomes will flow through.

  • Illinois uses a single sales factor, and many exporters benefit from foreign sales in the apportionment formula. Robust documentation of where customers are located and where benefit is received remains important.

  • Incentives and credits-such as EDGE credits or training grants-can partially offset investments that support FDII‑eligible export growth from a facility in or near Peru, Illinois.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do my foreign subsidiaries make me a US shareholder of a CFC, and will I owe GILTI?
You’re a US shareholder if you own (directly, indirectly, or constructively) at least 10% of a foreign corporation. If US shareholders collectively own over 50%, it’s a CFC and you generally must include GILTI annually. C corporations can reduce GILTI with a Section 250 deduction and indirect foreign tax credits; individuals may need a Section 962 election or a C‑corp blocker.
What are the most effective ways to reduce or defer GILTI?
Key levers include: electing the GILTI high‑tax exclusion for tested units taxed at least 18.9%; using a US C corporation (or a 962 election for individuals) to access the Section 250 deduction and indirect FTCs; aligning entity classification (check‑the‑box), expense timing, and intercompany pricing; increasing QBAI via tangible asset investment; and modeling country‑by‑country FTC limits.
Can we benefit from FDII and what does it require?
Only US C corporations qualify. FDII provides a Section 250 deduction on income from goods/services sold to foreign customers for foreign use (37.5% deduction through 2025; 21.875% thereafter). It requires documentation of foreign use, interacts with transfer pricing and supply chain, and isn’t available to pass‑throughs or individuals directly.
What filings and state considerations should we expect from Peru, Illinois?
Common federal filings are Forms 5471 (CFCs), 8992 (GILTI), 8993 (Section 250), 1118 (FTCs), and sometimes 8858/8865, due with your federal return (extensions available). Penalties for Form 5471 start at $10,000 per entity per year. State conformity varies; Illinois may require adjustments for GILTI/FDII and special apportionment—SALT modeling is essential.
