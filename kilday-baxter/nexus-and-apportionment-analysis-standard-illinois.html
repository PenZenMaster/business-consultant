Nexus and apportionment are the two pillars that determine whether, and how much, a multistate business owes to a state.

In Illinois, understanding both is essential, because the state's rules knit together modern notions of economic presence with long-standing sourcing principles that can yield very different results depending on a company's footprint and revenue model. A sound analysis starts by asking where Illinois may claim the right to tax you at all, and then turns to how much of your income or sales should be assigned to Illinois once that right exists.


Nexus in Illinois has two distinct tracks: transaction taxes such as sales and use taxes, and income-based taxes. For transaction taxes, the watershed moment was the Supreme Court's Wayfair decision in 2018, which untethered sales tax nexus from the need for physical presence. Like most states, Illinois moved quickly to implement “economic nexus” for remote sellers. As of recent years, remote retailers that exceed a statewide activity threshold-commonly framed as a dollar amount of Illinois sales or a number of separate transactions within a lookback period-are required to register and collect. Illinois also extends collection obligations to marketplace facilitators and requires destination sourcing for local tax rates under its Leveling the Playing Field rules. That means a remote sale shipped to a customer in Peoria is taxed at the Peoria rate, not the seller's home location. Practical triggers for sales tax nexus go beyond the economic threshold: storing inventory in an Illinois fulfillment center, maintaining in-state third-party repair or installation services, or sending personnel to trade shows can all establish physical presence nexus and accelerate collection duties even if you are below a numeric threshold.


For income-based taxes, Illinois blends federal constitutional standards, a federal statutory safe harbor, and state-level interpretations. Public Law 86-272 still shields an out-of-state seller of tangible personal property from state income tax when the seller's only in-state activity is the solicitation of orders approved and shipped from outside Illinois. Many common activities fall outside that protection, such as in-state services, post-sale support, warranty work performed in Illinois, or maintaining inventory in the state. The rise of digital commerce has complicated the line between protected solicitation and unprotected in-market activity. States around the country have updated their guidance on whether things like deploying internet cookies, offering in-app support, or providing remote diagnostic services to in-state customers cross the line; Illinois generally reads the safe harbor narrowly and businesses should review how their specific online and post-sale practices would be viewed today.


Beyond physical presence, Illinois has for years recognized economic presence as a basis for income tax nexus. By regulation, the Department of Revenue has adopted factor-presence style triggers that look to the magnitude of Illinois sales and other in-state contacts to assert nexus even if no boots are on the ground. While litigation and administrative guidance continue to shape the edges, the message is clear: large volumes of Illinois-bound revenue can, by themselves, create income tax filing obligations, subject to the limits of federal law and the P.L.

Once nexus exists, apportionment determines what slice of a multistate business's income is taxable by Illinois. Illinois uses a single sales factor apportionment formula for most industries, a policy choice meant to encourage in-state investment in people and property by minimizing the impact of payroll and property on the tax base. In short, the percentage of your business income taxed by Illinois equals your Illinois sales divided by your everywhere sales, calculated under the state's sourcing rules.


Sourcing is straightforward for sales of tangible personal property: the sale is in the Illinois numerator if the goods are delivered to a location in Illinois. A throwback rule can push some out-of-state shipments back into Illinois if the property ships from Illinois and the taxpayer is not taxable in the destination state. Companies with a hub or warehouse in Illinois should scrutinize throwback exposure; it can be a material driver of Illinois apportionment for wholesalers and manufacturers who sell widely into jurisdictions where they lack income tax nexus.


Services and receipts from intangibles require more judgment. Illinois uses market-based sourcing, asking where the customer received the benefit of the service rather than where the service provider performed the work. The statute and regulations supply a cascading set of rules and reasonable-approximation standards to pin down the market. For business-to-business services, that often means identifying the customer's location tied to the contract, the place where the benefit is actually realized, or, if the service relates to specific property, where that property is located. For consumer services, the customer's billing address can be a tiebreaker. For licensing intangibles or digital offerings such as software-as-a-service, Illinois again looks to the market, which can require a look-through to the user base in Illinois when the customer deploys the product to its own customers. Because Illinois relies on a facts-and-circumstances approach with fallback rules, careful documentation is vital to support whatever reasonable method you adopt to identify and quantify the Illinois benefit.


Apportionment in Illinois plays out at the group level for unitary combined filers. Illinois requires unitary combined reporting for corporate income tax, meaning commonly controlled entities engaged in a unitary business compute a single Illinois tax base, eliminate intercompany transactions, and then apply one apportionment factor based on the group's combined sales. The mechanics of group apportionment can cut both ways: a high-Illinois-sales member can drag the whole group's factor upward, while significant everywhere sales from other members can dilute the Illinois percentage. Partnerships and disregarded entities feed their apportionment factors up to their owners, which can shift the numerator and denominator in ways that surprise consolidated filers who have not mapped pass-through activity to Illinois's sourcing rules.


Industry-specific rules deserve a mention. Financial organizations, insurers, and certain transportation companies do not always use the standard single sales factor or apply it the same way; they often look to specialized receipts definitions and sourcing regimes. Likewise, construction contractors, broadcasters, and utilities may face tailored guidance on what counts as an Illinois receipt. If your revenue model departs from a plain-vanilla sale of goods or professional services, it is wise to check whether a special rule applies before defaulting to the general market-based framework.


A thorough nexus and apportionment analysis in Illinois typically follows a consistent playbook. Start by inventorying all Illinois touchpoints over the prior three to four years: employees, agents, property, inventory locations, trade shows, services performed in state, deliveries using your own vehicles, and remote sales volume. For sales tax, evaluate whether you met the economic threshold, acted through a marketplace, or created physical presence through inventory or service activities, and confirm you are collecting at the correct destination rates. For income tax, test each activity against P.L. 86-272 and identify any services or intangibles that pierce the safe harbor. Consider whether your Illinois sales volume independently triggers economic nexus under the state's rules.


Next, map revenue streams to Illinois's sourcing rules. Break out tangible goods, services, licensing, and digital or usage-based revenue. For each, establish a consistent, evidence-backed method to determine where the benefit is received. For tangible goods, quantify any throwback exposure. For services, line up contract data, delivery addresses, user logs, or other commercially maintained records that reasonably approximate Illinois usage. If you sell through multiple channels, confirm that marketplace and direct sales are properly segregated and sourced.


Finally, compute the Illinois sales factor on an annual basis, at the correct reporting level. For unitary groups, aggregate and eliminate intercompany transactions before apportioning. For pass-throughs, make sure owners receive the right Illinois factors.

Businesses that engage early with these questions, and that revisit them as their models change, are best positioned to manage both compliance risk and effective tax rate in Illinois.

Do Illinois throwback rules affect us?
Yes, for sales of tangible personal property shipped from Illinois to a state where you are not taxable (or to the U.S. government), receipts are “thrown back” to Illinois and included in the Illinois sales factor numerator. Throwback does not apply to services/intangibles. Verifying taxability in destination states can reduce throwback exposure.
