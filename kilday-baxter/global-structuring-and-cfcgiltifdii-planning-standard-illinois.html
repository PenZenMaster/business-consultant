Global structuring and CFC/GILTI/FDII planning Standard, Illinois

State and Federal Business Registration

Global structuring is no longer a “nice to have” for Illinois businesses; it is the operating system that determines how efficiently capital moves, where profits land, and how much tax you pay-federally, in foreign jurisdictions, and at home. For companies headquartered in Illinois, including those in smaller communities like Standard, a thoughtful approach to Controlled Foreign Corporations (CFCs) and the federal GILTI and FDII regimes can be the difference between compounding after‑tax cash flow and chronic leakage. The rules are complex, but the planning principles are straightforward: define where value is created, align that reality with your legal and tax footprint, and keep testing the model as laws evolve.


Start with the building blocks. A resilient global structure answers a handful of questions clearly. Which entities should own intellectual property and bear risk? Where are core functions performed and by whom? How is the group financed-intercompany debt, equity, or hybrids-and how do cash and earnings move? What documentation supports transfer pricing? And which jurisdictions provide substance, treaty access, and administrability without exposing you to unnecessary withholding, permanent establishment, or minimum tax top‑ups?

system, three federal constructs dominate the conversation for outbound groups: CFC/Subpart F, GILTI, and FDII.


CFC and Subpart F are the legacy guardrails. They pick off certain categories of highly mobile or passive income earned by foreign subsidiaries and require current U.S. inclusion by U.S. shareholders. Subpart F still matters; it interacts with today's planning for foreign financing, captive services, and holding structures, and it influences whether the newer high‑tax exceptions can shelter income at the CFC level.


GILTI, by contrast, is a broad, continuing inclusion intended to capture low‑taxed foreign earnings. In practice, GILTI is often the single largest driver of an Illinois company's effective tax rate on foreign operations.

The core mechanics are deceptively simple: aggregate tested income across CFCs, subtract a routine return on qualified tangible assets (QBAI), and include the balance currently in U.S. income. The artistry lives in the elections and interactions:



  • The high‑tax exclusion can remove high‑rate CFC income from GILTI altogether, but it is an all‑in, tested‑unit election with ripple effects for foreign tax credit positions and state outcomes.

  • Deemed paid foreign tax credits are limited to 80% for GILTI and cannot be carried forward or back. That cap makes timing, blending, and supply‑chain margin placement critical.

  • The section 250 deduction cuts the effective federal rate on GILTI for C corporations, but its percentage is scheduled to drop after 2025 absent legislative change. Modeling both current law and “post‑sunset” scenarios is essential for capital budgeting.

  • Pass‑through owners face a different calculus, often considering a section 962 election to approximate corporate treatment for GILTI. This can be powerful federally, yet state recognition of 962 is inconsistent and needs to be checked for Illinois purposes.


FDII is the flip side-a carrot to serve foreign markets from the United States. Properly documented income from the sale of goods, licensing, or services to foreign customers can benefit from a section 250 deduction, reducing your federal rate on those earnings. While FDII is not “free money”-it demands accurate customer location data, contract review, and careful pricing to avoid double counting-it meaningfully rewards U.S.‑based intangibles and export‑facing activity. Like GILTI's deduction, FDII's percentage is slated to decrease after 2025 if Congress does nothing, so do not rely on a static benefit curve.


Today's planning lives at the intersection of those rules and the global environment. OECD Pillar Two minimum taxes, expanding substance requirements abroad, and the U.S. book‑minimum tax all push structures toward simplification, transparency, and genuine business alignment. The practical effect: fewer brass‑plate entities, more on‑the‑ground capability where revenue is earned, and tighter documentation tying functions, assets, and risks to outcomes. That does not eliminate tax planning. It refocuses it on capital structure, supply‑chain margins, IP lifecycle management, and election strategy rather than chasing arbitrage that no longer lasts.


If you are operating from Illinois, add a state lens before you finalize the blueprint. Illinois generally uses federal taxable income as its corporate starting point, with modifications and its own apportionment rules. That means:



  • The federal outcome for GILTI and FDII often flows into the Illinois base, but the state may require additions, subtractions, or different treatments for foreign‑source dividends, GILTI‑like inclusions, and special deductions. The Department of Revenue's guidance and legislative changes have evolved since 2018; if you have not refreshed your state model recently, assume your old answer is stale.

  • Apportionment matters. Illinois uses a single sales factor for most taxpayers and market‑based sourcing for services and many intangibles. Whether a given foreign receipt sits in the sales factor-and where it is sourced-can swing your Illinois liability significantly. GILTI, in particular, is not a garden‑variety sale; confirm how it is treated in both the base and the sales factor.

  • The combined Illinois corporate income tax and personal property replacement tax is material. For C corporations, the statutory corporate rate plus the replacement tax results in a combined burden that frequently lands around the high single digits. For S corporations and partnerships, the replacement tax applies even though corporate income tax does not. These layers should be explicitly modeled alongside your federal results.

  • Section 962 elections, foreign tax credits, and section 250 deductions may not be mirrored at the state level the way they are federally. If you are a pass‑through owner considering a 962 election to blunt GILTI, verify whether Illinois will respect the election and how it flows to the state base.

  • Illinois incentive programs and credits-such as the state R&D credit in some years or project‑specific incentives-can be decisive when comparing keeping more functions in‑state versus offshoring them. FDII can make a U.S.‑centric footprint more attractive; state incentives can tip that balance further.


With those contours, what does good planning look like for an Illinois business?


First, map the business reality. Catalogue each legal entity, its functions, headcount, tangible assets, IP, intercompany agreements, and customer locations. Many companies discover that their legal chart tells one story while people, contracts, and cash tell another. Tax follows substance-fix the gaps.


Second, model before you move. Run multiyear scenarios under current federal law, post‑2025 deduction sunsets, plausible foreign rate changes, and Illinois treatments. Stress‑test the GILTI high‑tax election, the effect of moving or consolidating CFCs, the use of check‑the‑box to simplify chains, and the placement of IP relative to FDII. Layer in foreign tax credit outcomes with and without baskets leaking credits due to the 80% limitation.


Third, align transfer pricing with the structure you actually want. If FDII is a goal, ensure the U.S. principal retains non‑routine returns commensurate with its DEMPE functions over intangibles. If you expect to rely on the GILTI high‑tax exclusion, avoid isolating low‑taxed pockets of income that poison the pool. Document intercompany services, cost‑sharing or licensing, and limited‑risk distributions with contemporary benchmarking.


Fourth, be intentional with capital.

Thin‑capitalize where it is defensible, but don't let interest limitations or anti‑hybrid rules wipe out the benefit. Consider local withholding and treaty access when deciding whether to push cash up as dividends, interest, or royalties. Repatriation is simpler post‑TCJA, yet local law, minimum taxes, and bank covenants still create friction.


Fifth, get the Illinois details right. Confirm the current treatment of GILTI and foreign‑source dividends in your Illinois return, how any section 250 deduction flows, and how your foreign receipts impact the sales factor. Reconcile the Illinois return to your federal 8992 and 8993 workpapers; inconsistencies are easy audit targets. Watch for legislative updates near fiscal year ends, when conformity tweaks and NOL caps often appear.


Finally, operationalize compliance. Build a data spine that supports Forms 5471, 8992, 8993, 1118, and country‑by‑country reporting, and that can be sliced for Illinois apportionment and addback schedules. The cheapest tax planning is avoiding adjustments because your data cannot prove what you say is true.


Companies in and around Standard, Illinois, do not need sprawling legal structures to compete globally. They need clear objectives, a structure that matches where and how they create value, and a disciplined cadence of modeling and maintenance as the rules evolve. When you get CFC, GILTI, and FDII planning right-and integrate Illinois' rules rather than treating them as an afterthought-you compress your effective tax rate, improve cash predictability, and give your leadership more degrees of freedom to reinvest.


The theme is simple: build for substance, elect with intention, and keep your federal, foreign, and Illinois positions in one integrated model. Do that, and international expansion becomes a tailwind rather than a tax risk.

Frequently Asked Questions

How should we structure our foreign operations to limit CFC/Subpart F and GILTI exposure without disrupting the business?
Centralize ownership under a U.S. parent, use check‑the‑box where appropriate to collapse passive flows, align transfer pricing to keep income active, elect the GILTI high‑tax exclusion when effective, optimize expense allocation and QBAI, and blend high‑ and low‑tax CFC income—always after modeling cash tax and ETR impacts.
How can we practically reduce GILTI and maximize FDII benefits?
For GILTI, use the high‑tax exclusion, optimize foreign tax credits (including expense allocation and basket limits), balance tested income/loss across CFCs, and consider entity classification changes. For FDII, document foreign sales/services and foreign use, manage U.S. QBAI and margins, coordinate IP location and royalty flows, claim the Section 250 deduction, and monitor the scheduled post‑2025 deduction percentage changes.
What should an Illinois‑based company expect at the state level for GILTI/FDII?
Illinois generally starts from federal taxable income, so GILTI and the Section 250 deduction can flow into the state base, but state adjustments, apportionment, and any foreign‑dividend subtractions can materially change results. Model Illinois (and other states) separately to confirm inclusion/exclusion, sourcing, and conformity, as rules differ and are subject to change.
