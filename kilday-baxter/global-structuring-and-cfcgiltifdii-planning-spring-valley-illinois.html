Global structuring and CFC/GILTI/FDII planning Spring Valley, Illinois

State and Federal Business Registration

Global business no longer belongs only to big-city multinationals. In and around Spring Valley, Illinois, manufacturers, distributors, ag businesses, and technology firms are winning customers in Canada, Europe, Latin America, and Asia. With that growth comes a tangle of international tax rules that can either quietly protect margins or just as quietly erode them. Three concepts matter more than most in today's landscape: how you structure foreign operations, how you plan for CFC and GILTI, and how you leverage FDII. Getting those right, in a way that fits the character and capital of a Spring Valley enterprise, can be the difference between an overseas venture that compounds value and one that adds complexity without reward.


Start with structural clarity. The best global structures flow from the business model, not the other way around. Where are your customers, where is your production, which entity owns the key intangibles, and how do cash and decisions move? A simple, documented answer to those questions almost always beats an ornate chart of companies.

In practice, that means choosing the fewest number of foreign entities that can responsibly manage local risk, staffing, and tax registration needs, while keeping legal ownership of valuable intellectual property and strategic contracts in a place that aligns with your long-term goals. For many midwestern companies, that place is still the United States, because U.S. tax rules now include incentives to keep exports and know‑how onshore.


Controlled Foreign Corporation rules sit at the core of U.S. international taxation for closely held and middle‑market businesses. A foreign subsidiary can be a CFC if more than 50 percent is owned by U.S. shareholders with at least 10 percent interests. Once you have a CFC, you have to think about two income categories: older Subpart F income, which mostly targets passive or mobile income, and the newer GILTI regime, which picks up the residual “tested” profits of CFCs. GILTI is designed to discourage shifting high‑return activities into low‑tax jurisdictions. For U.S. C‑corporations, GILTI often carries a favorable deduction and partial foreign tax credits, producing an effective rate that can be manageable if the foreign taxes paid are moderate to high. For individuals and S‑corporation owners, by contrast, GILTI can be harsh unless you plan. That is why the 962 election exists: it lets an individual be taxed on GILTI as if through a C‑corp, unlocking a deduction and credits, at the cost of a potential second layer of tax if cash is later distributed. In owner‑managed companies so common across Bureau County, this election can be the difference between a sustainable global footprint and an unwelcome April surprise.


Within GILTI, a handful of levers are consistently powerful. The high‑tax exclusion allows you to opt out of GILTI for CFC income already taxed abroad above a certain threshold, but it requires consistency across related entities and careful year‑by‑year modeling. The way you classify entities-using so‑called “check‑the‑box” elections-can consolidate profits and losses across affiliates, reducing exposed income. The foreign tax credit mechanics are intricate: credits for GILTI are capped, cannot be carried forward, and are sensitive to how you allocate U.S. interest expense. Small changes in capital structure, intercompany lending, and cash pooling can ripple through those calculations. This is why a tidy diagram of your entities, intercompany agreements that reflect real economics, and a calendar of elections and filings are not paperwork-they are value protection.


If GILTI is the stick, FDII is the carrot. FDII rewards U.S. C‑corporations that earn income from exporting goods, services, and the use of intangibles to foreign customers. It effectively lowers the tax rate on those foreign‑derived profits, subject to rules that prevent abuse. For a Spring Valley manufacturer shipping engineered components to Europe, or a software firm licensing tools to clients in Canada, FDII can justify keeping core intellectual property and key staff in the U.S.

rather than moving them abroad. The catch is that FDII belongs only to C‑corporations, not to S‑corps or partnerships. So the entity form you chose years ago for domestic reasons may not be the one that maximizes today's cross‑border incentives. Some companies respond with a holding‑company structure that includes a C‑corp exporter alongside a pass‑through domestic operating business. Others consider converting to C‑corp status, weighing FDII and capital needs against the reality of double taxation on distributions. There is no one right answer; there is only the answer that fits your growth plan, exit horizon, and shareholders' tax positions.


Bringing CFC/GILTI and FDII together is where practical planning lives. Consider three common Spring Valley scenarios. A second‑generation tool‑and‑die shop opens a small plant in Mexico to be near a customer, generating profits taxed at a moderate local rate. With a well‑timed high‑tax exclusion election and a 962 strategy for the owners, those profits can avoid punitive U.S. inclusions while still allowing tax‑efficient repatriation when cash is needed. A seed technology startup begins licensing software to agribusiness clients in Brazil and Australia while keeping development in Illinois. Housing the IP in a U.S. C‑corp exporter may unlock FDII benefits without sacrificing control over the code or stretching management capacity overseas. A logistics company buys a United Kingdom distributor. Instead of building a chain of holding companies, it keeps the UK subsidiary straightforward, ensures intercompany pricing is documented, and models whether paying a modest dividend back to a U.S. C‑corp to use the participation exemption makes more sense than leaving cash parked abroad.


Illinois adds a local dimension worth noting. The state uses a single‑sales‑factor formula for most corporations, which often means that an uptick in foreign sales does not raise the Illinois income tax base the way it once might have. Corporate tax in Illinois is effectively 9.5 percent when you include the replacement tax, so modeling whether profits should sit in a U.S. C‑corp (to claim FDII) or at the shareholder level (to preserve S‑corp benefits) is an Illinois‑specific exercise, not just a federal one. Many family‑owned companies here are S‑corps out of habit and history; that habit deserves a fresh look when foreign customers and subsidiaries enter the picture. Meanwhile, Illinois' pass‑through entity tax election can help owners recover some state and local tax limitations at the federal level, improving the net economics of staying pass‑through if FDII is not a compelling driver.


The global rules keep changing. Countries are adopting a 15 percent global minimum tax under the OECD's Pillar Two framework. While the U.S. has not conformed, several of our trading partners have, and that can affect how much tax your foreign subsidiaries pay and where. Transfer pricing expectations are higher almost everywhere, and penalties for thin documentation can be severe. Even for a modest Spring Valley operation, a contemporaneous memo that explains why your intercompany prices are arm's length is cheap insurance. Banking and treasury matter too: predictable hedging, clear dividend policies, and avoiding surprise withholding taxes on cross‑border payments can all protect earnings you worked hard to win.


If you are starting or expanding abroad this year, a practical playbook helps. Map your current and planned legal entities, ownership, and intercompany flows. Identify where Subpart F or GILTI might arise, and whether the high‑tax exclusion could apply. Decide if any owners should make a 962 election. If FDII could be material, evaluate whether a C‑corp exporter makes sense and confirm that your contracts and invoicing support foreign‑use requirements. Review transfer pricing and prepare documentation before year‑end. Model foreign tax credits and U.S. expense allocations to avoid leaving credits stranded. Align the Illinois picture, including apportionment, replacement tax, and any pass‑through elections. Finally, organize compliance: Forms 5471 for CFCs, 8992 for GILTI, 8993 for the FDII and GILTI deduction, and the foreign tax credit schedules. These forms are not glamorous, but they are how good planning survives contact with the real world.


At heart, global structuring and CFC/GILTI/FDII planning is not about clever diagrams.
