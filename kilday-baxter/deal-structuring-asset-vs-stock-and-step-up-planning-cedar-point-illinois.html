Deal structuring (asset vs. stock) and step-up planning Cedar Point, Illinois

Kilday, Baxter & Associates LLC

Deal structuring is never just a tax question or just a legal question. It's the intersection where tax economics, liability management, financing, and practical realities meet. In a small-market setting like Cedar Point, Illinois-where many deals involve closely held companies, family transitions, and a tight-knit network of employees and vendors-the way you choose between an asset deal and a stock deal, and how you plan for a tax basis step-up, can make a multi–six-figure difference in after-tax proceeds and cash flow.


Asset versus stock: the core trade-offs



  • Liability and cleanliness: Buyers usually prefer asset purchases because they can pick the assets they want and leave behind most liabilities. That said, certain liabilities can follow the assets under law (environmental, payroll and sales/use tax successor liabilities, employee claims), so diligence and indemnities still matter. In a stock deal, the company continues as is-contracts, permits, and liabilities-just with new owners. This is simpler operationally but carries more historical risk for the buyer.

  • Consents and continuity: Asset deals often require assigning contracts, leases, permits, and vendor accounts, each of which may need third-party consent. That can slow closing and complicate relationships. Stock deals avoid much of that friction because the legal entity doesn't change, though change-of-control clauses can still bite.

  • Taxes for buyers: An asset deal naturally gives buyers a step-up in tax basis-meaning they can depreciate and amortize assets from the new, higher purchase price. That improves post-closing cash flow. In a stock deal, the target's asset basis usually carries over unchanged, unless you use special elections to create a deemed asset sale.

  • Taxes for sellers: Sellers generally prefer stock sales. A C corporation selling assets can face corporate-level tax and then shareholder-level tax on a distribution-two layers. S corporation or LLC/partnership sellers might still face ordinary income recapture on depreciation, but overall they often prefer stock for simplicity and potential capital gain treatment. The push and pull between buyer and seller benefits is where pricing “gross-up” negotiations start.


Illinois and LaSalle County wrinkles to know



  • Successor tax liability and notice: Illinois can hold buyers liable for a seller's unpaid sales and use tax, withholding, and certain other state taxes. To protect yourself, file Form CBS-1 (Notice of Sale, Purchase, or Transfer of Business Assets) with the Illinois Department of Revenue at least 10 business days before closing. This is critical-don't skip it.

  • Sales and use tax on tangible assets: Illinois generally provides an occasional sale exemption for certain nonrecurring sales of business assets, but its application is fact-specific and does not cover all situations (for example, motor vehicles and registered equipment bring separate use/registration tax considerations). Analyze equipment, vehicles, and inventory separately and get written guidance if needed.

  • Real estate transfer taxes: If the deal includes real property, Illinois imposes a state transfer tax (generally $0.50 per $500 of value) and counties typically impose $0.25 per $500, with some municipalities adding their own amounts. LaSalle County follows the standard county tax. Budget for recording fees and title policy costs too.

  • Corporate income and replacement taxes: C corporations pay Illinois corporate income tax and a Personal Property Replacement Tax; the combined burden is commonly referenced around 9.5% for C corps (7% corporate income tax plus 2.5% replacement tax). S corporations and partnerships do not pay the 7% entity-level income tax, but generally owe a 1.5% replacement tax on Illinois income and may have withholding obligations for nonresident owners.

  • Depreciation decoupling: Illinois often decouples from federal 168(k) bonus depreciation. That means a buyer's federal tax shield from a step-up can be front-loaded, while the Illinois deduction may be spread over time. Model both sets of rules so your cash flow projections are realistic.

  • Unemployment insurance experience: If you buy substantially all of a business's assets, you may inherit the seller's Illinois unemployment insurance experience rate. In a pure stock deal, the rate generally stays with the company. Confirm this early with the Illinois Department of Employment Security to avoid surprises in payroll budgets.


Building the step-up: how structure drives outcomes



  • Straight asset purchase: The simplest path to a step-up. Buyers allocate the purchase price across assets under the federal residual method (Section 1060), documented on IRS Form 8594. Goodwill and going-concern value become Section 197 intangibles amortizable over 15 years. Tangible assets follow their own class lives. Make sure the valuation and allocation are defensible-this drives future tax deductions and affects the seller's tax character (ordinary recapture versus capital gain).

  • Stock purchase with a deemed asset sale: If the target is a corporation, a Section 338(h)(10) election (for S corps and certain subsidiaries) or a Section 336(e) election can give the buyer a basis step-up without having to retitle every asset. For C corps where 338(h)(10) is not available, a Section 338(g) election may be used, but it imposes tax at the corporate level-often deal-breaking unless priced in. These elections require careful coordination and timely filings.

  • LLCs and partnerships: If you buy 100% of the equity of a multi-member LLC taxed as a partnership, the transaction is generally treated as an asset acquisition for federal tax purposes by operation of law. Alternatively, partial interest acquisitions can use a Section 754 election to step up inside basis for the new investor under Section 743(b). These mechanics often deliver the buyer-friendly step-up outcome while keeping the entity intact.

  • Stock purchase without an election: The buyer gets no step-up. This can still be attractive if liabilities are well understood, contracts are sensitive to assignment, or the purchase price already reflects the lost tax shield.


Modeling the economics
A practical rule of thumb is to translate tax attributes into price. If an asset deal (or a deemed asset deal via election) creates, say, $2 million of present-value tax savings for the buyer, the seller will often ask for a higher price to share that benefit. Conversely, if a stock sale spares a C corp seller from double tax, the buyer may insist on a discount to offset the missing step-up. Optimal deals make the pie bigger with tax planning, then cut it in a way both sides can live with.


Key tax items to model before term sheets are final:



  • Depreciation and amortization schedules for stepped-up assets, including Illinois decoupling effects.

  • Ordinary income recapture on tangible assets and amortizable intangibles for the seller.

  • Entity-level taxes: C corp double tax risk; S corp built-in gains exposure if the S election is recent; Illinois replacement tax.

Local execution items around Cedar Point



  • Title and environmental diligence: LaSalle County has a long industrial and quarrying history. If you're buying real property or equipment with potential environmental exposure, phase I (and possibly phase II) assessments are table stakes. Environmental liabilities can follow the asset buyer.

  • Licenses and permits: Check state-level permits, sales tax registration, withholding accounts, motor fuel or specialized industry licenses, and any village or county permits. In an asset deal, you may need new accounts and fresh registrations.

  • Vendor, customer, and landlord consents: In smaller markets, relationships matter as much as contracts.

Family transitions and estate step-up planning
Many Cedar Point-area businesses are family-owned. Two “step-ups” matter here:



  • Deal-driven step-up: Achieved through asset deals, deemed asset elections, or partnership basis adjustments. This is about the buyer's future deductions.

  • Estate step-up at death: When an owner dies holding appreciated assets (including interests in LLCs and S corps), heirs often receive a basis step-up to fair market value for federal purposes. Illinois has its own estate tax with a $4 million exemption, lower than the federal threshold, which can influence timing and structure. Coordinating lifetime sales, redemptions, or freeze partnerships with estate plans can increase basis, reduce state estate tax, or both.


For family handoffs, consider:



  • Conversions to LLCs taxed as partnerships to enable future 754 step-ups as interests change hands.

  • Installment sales to irrevocable grantor trusts for estate freezing, paired with deliberate timing of 754 elections.

  • Buy-sell agreements that set valuation mechanics acceptable to both tax and estate administrators.


A simple illustration
Imagine acquiring a manufacturing business near Cedar Point for $8 million, including $2 million of equipment (much of it fully depreciated), $1 million of inventory, $1 million of real estate, and $4 million of intangibles and goodwill.



  • Asset deal result: Buyer allocates price via Form 8594. The real estate gets a new basis and is depreciated; equipment gets new basis with potential federal bonus or accelerated depreciation; goodwill amortizes over 15 years. Federal tax shields could easily exceed $1.5–$2 million in present value, depending on the mix and your tax rate.

  • Stock deal with 338(h)(10): Buyer gets a deemed asset step-up without retitling everything, subject to the seller's tax costs. If the seller is an S corp, the parties often trade price for the buyer's tax benefits, landing somewhere between the pure stock and pure asset economics.


Practical steps to get it right



  • Decide early which structure you want and why. Use a side-by-side model to compare after-tax cash outcomes for both parties.

  • Lock in the purchase price allocation in the LOI if possible, at least at the asset class level. Consistency between buyer and seller is required.

  • Run Illinois-specific tax projections, including replacement tax and depreciation decoupling, not just federal.

  • File CBS-1 on time and get comfort on seller tax clearance; build escrows where clearance is incomplete.

  • Map out contract assignments and consents before the definitive agreement is signed.

  • Confirm unemployment insurance rate impacts with IDES and budget them.

  • If using 338(h)(10), 336(e), or 754, calendar the elections and filing requirements and assign responsibility for execution.


In a market like Cedar Point, where reputation and continuity are vital, the best deal often blends technical tax efficiency with practical sensitivity to employees, customers, and landlords. Asset versus stock is the headline, but step-up planning is the lever that quietly moves the numbers. Use it deliberately, price it transparently, and execute it locally with care. And because the Illinois details can swing the outcome, involve an Illinois-focused M&A tax advisor and local counsel early enough to shape the LOI-waiting until the purchase agreement stage is usually too late to capture the full value.
