Deal structuring (asset vs. stock) and step-up planning Peru, Illinois

Oglesby, IL 61348

If you're buying or selling a closely held business in Peru, Illinois, one of the earliest choices you'll face is how to structure the deal: as a sale of assets or a sale of equity (often called a stock sale). That choice shapes not only taxes, but also liability, financing, working-capital needs, which licenses and contracts survive, and how smoothly Day 1 operations will run. Layer onto that the question of whether the buyer can secure a “step-up” in the tax basis of the acquired assets, and you have the heart of the economic negotiation.


What an asset deal really means
In an asset purchase, the buyer selects specific assets to acquire and liabilities to assume. For a manufacturer or distributor along the I‑80 corridor near Peru, that could mean equipment, inventory, customer lists, trademarks, the plant or warehouse, and certain contracts, while leaving behind unwanted liabilities. Tax-wise, the buyer generally receives a step-up in the basis of the acquired assets up to the purchase price, allocated among asset classes under federal rules. That step-up drives future depreciation and amortization deductions, including 15‑year amortization of many intangibles like goodwill and certain customer relationships. The seller, however, may face less favorable tax treatment on some asset categories (for example, ordinary income recapture on depreciated equipment), so the “price” for getting the buyer a step-up is often paid in the form of a higher purchase price or a tax gross-up.


Operationally, asset deals require more work: titles transfer, vendor and customer contracts may need consents, permits may need to be reissued, and employees are formally rehired by the buyer.

But the ability to avoid unknown legacy liabilities can be worth the effort, especially in industries where product liability, environmental exposure, or sales/use tax compliance risks can trail the target.


What a stock deal really means
In a stock purchase, the buyer acquires the company as a whole-assets, liabilities, and history included. Day‑to‑day continuity is usually smoother: contracts, permits, and employees remain with the same legal entity, and counterparties may not need to consent. Sellers tend to prefer stock deals because gain is often taxed at capital gains rates and there's less risk of ordinary income recapture.


The downside for buyers is that the tax basis of the company's assets generally does not change. You inherit the target's historical asset basis and its future deductions timetable. You also inherit potential exposures-tax, legal, environmental-that your diligence may or may not fully surface. Buyers typically push for strong indemnities, escrows, and representation-and-warranty insurance to bridge that gap.


How to get a step-up in a stock deal
If you like the operational ease of a stock deal but still want the tax benefits of an asset step-up, federal law offers a few paths:




  • Section 338(h)(10) election: Available for S corporations and certain C‑corp subsidiary sales, this lets the parties treat a qualifying stock sale as an asset sale for tax purposes. The company itself is treated as having sold its assets and then liquidated. Buyers get the step-up; sellers face the tax as if assets were sold.




  • Section 336(e) election: Functionally similar to 338(h)(10) in certain circumstances, offering an alternative when 338(h)(10) isn't available.




  • Section 338(g) election: Buyer‑only election that delivers a step-up, but the target bears the deemed asset sale tax. It can be costly and requires careful pricing.




For partnerships and LLCs taxed as partnerships, a different toolkit applies:



  • Section 754 election: When an interest transfers, a 754 election enables a 743(b) adjustment that steps up the inside basis of partnership assets allocable to the buyer. If there's a distribution, a 734(b) adjustment can also step up basis. This is often the most efficient “step-up planning” lever for deals involving LLCs.


Why the step-up is so valuable
The step-up converts purchase price into future deductions, improving cash flow and effective yield on the deal. A simple illustration: If $10 million of purchase price is allocated to amortizable intangibles, that produces roughly $666,667 of annual amortization deductions over 15 years. At a 25% blended tax rate (purely illustrative), that's about $166,667 of annual cash tax savings before time value.

Illinois layers you cannot ignore
Illinois generally starts from federal tax results, including recognition of 338 and 336(e) elections and partnership 754 adjustments, but it also imposes its own wrinkles. Two deserve special attention:




  • Personal Property Replacement Tax (PPRT): Illinois imposes a replacement tax on business entities in addition to the state income tax. The combined state burden and how it interacts with step-up deductions affects your modeling. Confirm current rates by entity type and whether any special credits or addbacks apply to your fact pattern.




  • Conformity to federal depreciation: Illinois has, at times, decoupled from federal bonus depreciation and made other adjustments. Your purchase price allocation (and any plan to lean heavily on short‑lived assets) should be modeled under both federal and Illinois rules so you are not surprised at state cash taxes.




Peru, LaSalle County, and practical state compliance
Beyond the tax math, Illinois has practical requirements whenever “business assets” change hands:




  • Successor liability and the bulk sale notice: Illinois can hold buyers liable for a seller's unpaid sales and use tax, withholding, and other business taxes tied to transferred assets. File the state's bulk sale notification (Form CBS‑1) at least 10 business days before closing, and hold back an escrow if the Illinois Department of Revenue signals exposure. Skipping this step can turn an otherwise clean asset deal into an expensive lesson.




  • Sales tax on asset transfers: Many transfers of a going concern qualify for an occasional sale exemption, but it's not automatic. Inventory, vehicles, and certain equipment categories can have special rules. Verify with your tax advisor which assets qualify and ensure invoices and bills of sale are drafted to preserve the exemption where applicable.




  • Unemployment insurance experience rating: If you acquire “substantially all” of a business, the Illinois Department of Employment Security may transfer the seller's unemployment experience (and any delinquencies). This can change your future payroll tax costs. Plan for it in the quality-of-earnings work.

    Negotiating the asset vs. stock trade
    Buyers and sellers in Illinois often land on a few well‑worn compromises:




    • Price vs. tax form: If the buyer demands an asset deal or a 338(h)(10) election to secure a step‑up, the seller asks for a price increase or a tax gross‑up to offset ordinary income and double‑tax costs.




    • Allocation matters: The parties file Form 8594 to allocate price among asset classes. Buyers push toward shorter‑lived assets and amortizable intangibles; sellers push toward favorable capital gain items like goodwill. The allocation is binding on both sides once filed, so treat it like part of the price.




    • Indemnities and escrows: Stock deals typically feature broader indemnities for pre‑closing taxes and legacy liabilities, with an escrow or rep‑and‑warranty insurance backstopping the risk. Asset deals still need targeted indemnities-environmental, product liability, open audits-but buyers usually accept a narrower scope.




    • Earnouts and rollover equity: If tax form is stuck, earnouts or seller rollover equity can bridge valuation gaps while letting the buyer secure a step‑up via an asset form or election.

    • Verify sales/use tax treatment of each asset class; segregate inventory and vehicles.

    • Scrub environmental conditions at any manufacturing or warehouse site; tailor indemnities.

    • Map permits and licenses that must be reissued versus those that ride through in a stock deal.

    • Coordinate with IDES on unemployment experience transfer.

    • Engage the LaSalle County Recorder early for real estate recording requirements and transfer taxes; check whether any municipal transfer taxes apply.

    • Validate TIF, enterprise zone, or other local incentives and whether a change of control requires consent to keep them.

    • Model federal and Illinois cash taxes under asset, stock, and hybrid elections; reflect PPRT and any depreciation conformity differences.

    • Lock down the purchase price allocation before filing deadlines; maintain support files.

    • Calendar post‑closing registrations with the Illinois Department of Revenue and local authorities and update withholding, sales tax, and unemployment accounts.


    Putting it all together
    In Peru, Illinois, the “right” structure is the one that protects the buyer from hidden liabilities, preserves operational continuity, and optimizes after‑tax cash flow for both sides. Asset deals and step‑up planning deliver compelling tax shields but require extra closing work and, often, a higher sticker price. Stock deals ease operational friction but shift more risk to indemnities and insurance and leave tax benefits on the table-unless you use elections like 338(h)(10), 336(e), or a 754 step‑up for partnerships.


    The best results come when both sides model federal and Illinois taxes in parallel, address Illinois successor liability with proper notice, and involve local officials early to preserve incentives. With that groundwork laid, you can focus on what matters most on Day 1 in Peru: keeping customers, employees, and production running-and letting the tax structure quietly do its job in the background.

    Frequently Asked Questions

    As a buyer in Peru, Illinois, is an asset purchase or a stock purchase usually better for taxes?
    Buyers usually prefer asset deals because they get a step-up in the assets’ basis (including goodwill amortizable over 15 years), faster depreciation, and can avoid unwanted liabilities. Stock deals are simpler operationally but generally don’t provide an inside basis step-up without special elections. Illinois broadly conforms to federal rules; no local Peru income tax applies.
    How can we get a step-up in a stock deal?
    Use a deemed-asset election. For corporations, consider a Section 338(h)(10) (S corps or certain consolidated C corps) or 336(e) election; file timely (generally by the 15th day of the 9th month after acquisition). For LLCs/partnerships, make a Section 754 election to give the buyer a 743(b) step-up. Buying a disregarded SMLLC is already treated as an asset purchase for tax.
    What Illinois-specific issues should we watch in asset vs. stock deals?
    State taxes: 4.95% individual rate; C-corp effective 9.5% (7% corporate + 2.5% replacement tax); S corps/partnerships owe 1.5% replacement tax. File Illinois Form CBS-1 (bulk sale notice) at least 10 business days pre-closing to avoid successor liability for seller’s IL taxes. Most bulk sales of business assets qualify for Illinois’ occasional sale exemption (no sales tax), but inventory sales remain taxable. No Peru city income tax; apportionment may source asset-sale business income to Illinois.
    How should we allocate the purchase price, and what forms are required?
    Agree on a Section 1060 allocation across asset classes; both parties must file consistent IRS Form 8594 for asset or deemed-asset deals (including 338 elections). Allocation drives future depreciation/amortization for the buyer and character of seller’s gain. Illinois follows the federal allocation.
    As a seller, which structure is usually more tax-efficient, and can I defer tax?
    Sellers often prefer stock sales for capital-gain treatment and, for C corps, to avoid entity-level tax on asset gains. In an S corp, an asset sale flows through to owners (watch built-in gains tax if within the 5-year BIG period after a C-to-S conversion). Deferral/reduction tools include installment sales (except on depreciation recapture), earnouts, residency planning, and QSBS (if eligible). Illinois taxes residents on all income (4.95%); nonresidents generally aren’t taxed by Illinois on stock-sale gains.
