Deal structuring (asset vs. stock) and step-up planning Spring Valley, Illinois

Julie Kilday, CPA

When owners and buyers sit down to structure a business sale in Spring Valley, Illinois, the first big fork in the road is usually asset purchase versus stock (or membership interest) purchase. The choice is not just a matter of paperwork; it drives taxes, liability, financing, post-closing operations, and even how the community experiences the transition. Layered onto that decision is step‑up planning-the set of elections and allocations that determine whether the buyer receives a higher tax basis in the assets, which affects future depreciation, amortization, and cash flow. Getting these two pieces right can be the difference between a deal that hums from day one and one that drags under avoidable costs.


In an asset sale, the buyer selects and acquires specific assets-equipment, inventory, receivables, trade name, customer lists, sometimes real estate-and usually leaves behind unwanted liabilities. Because the purchase price is allocated across asset classes under the federal residual method, the buyer gets a basis step‑up to the amounts paid. That basis can be depreciated or amortized, improving after‑tax returns. Sellers, by contrast, may face a mix of capital gain and ordinary income, especially where depreciation recapture or noncompete payments are involved. In Illinois, the asset sale also brings some uniquely local mechanics: before closing on a transfer of business assets, you generally need to submit a bulk sale notice to the Illinois Department of Revenue. The state can issue a claim for unpaid sales, use, withholding, or other business taxes; if you skip this step, the buyer can inherit successor liability. In practice, getting this notice in at least 10 business days before closing helps everyone sleep at night and is a standard checklist item for closings in Bureau County and beyond.


A stock deal is simpler on paper. The buyer acquires the company as a whole, including its history, contracts, and liabilities, unless excluded by agreement or refinanced. The business keeps operating without re‑titling every asset, which can be a blessing in industries with many permits or customer consents. For the seller, the tax result is often cleaner, especially for individuals: the gain is typically capital, and for nonresidents selling stock in an Illinois company, the gain may not be Illinois‑source income. That last point can matter if the ownership sits outside the state; nonresident shareholders often favor stock sales partly for this reason. The tradeoff is that a plain vanilla stock purchase does not give the buyer an inside basis step‑up in the assets, which means less future depreciation and amortization. Step‑up planning fills that gap in some cases.


The main step‑up tools vary by entity type. For corporations, an Internal Revenue Code section 338(h)(10) election (or its cousin, section 336(e)) can treat a qualifying stock sale as if the corporation sold its assets and then liquidated, yielding a buyer step‑up with the tax paid at the seller level.

This is common with S corporations and with certain C corporations in consolidated groups. Buyers love it because they receive basis in the assets; sellers weigh the tax bill from the deemed asset sale versus the premium they can demand for granting the election. For partnerships and LLCs taxed as partnerships, a section 754 election can deliver a targeted step‑up for the buyer's share of the assets when interests change hands. In each case, Illinois generally conforms to these federal basis rules, so the step‑up you engineer at the federal level carries through for Illinois income tax.


Local realities influence which path works best in Spring Valley. Many transactions here are owner‑operated businesses with real property, equipment, and a loyal workforce. Community banks familiar with the area often finance acquisitions, and lenders tend to prefer asset deals because they can take liens directly on inventories, fixtures, and accounts. If real estate is part of the transaction, expect state and county real estate transfer taxes at closing and be prepared for title, survey, and zoning diligence with Bureau County. Spring Valley does not impose the kind of steep municipal transfer taxes you see in Chicago, but checking current rates before term sheet stage avoids surprises.


On the tax front, a few patterns routinely shape negotiations. Buyers typically push for asset deals or for stock deals accompanied by a 338(h)(10)/336(e) election to secure the step‑up. Sellers weigh the higher effective tax they might incur on an asset sale-especially if there is significant depreciation recapture-against any purchase price premium or indemnity relief the buyer offers. Nonresident owners of Illinois pass‑throughs should be acutely aware that an asset sale (including a 338(h)(10)) generates Illinois‑source income to the extent of the business's Illinois apportionment, while a stock sale may not. That single difference can swing after‑tax proceeds substantially, so it is common to run side‑by‑side models early.


Compliance and successor risk also cut differently by structure. Asset deals demand careful attention to the Illinois bulk sale notice, to sales and use tax exposure on tangible personal property, and to unemployment insurance and withholding clearances with the state. Some assets-like vehicles-carry title transfer requirements and possible taxes or fees at the Secretary of State. Licenses for food service, health, or liquor are often municipal, and in Spring Valley you should confirm transferability and timing with City Hall well in advance. In a stock deal, the licenses may remain in place but the buyer must understand and price historical risks: unpaid taxes, wage and hour claims, environmental matters if there is legacy manufacturing or fuel storage, and any union contracts or benefit plan liabilities. Well‑drafted indemnities, escrows, and sometimes representation and warranty insurance help bridge those issues.


Step‑up planning is not just about elections; it is also about allocation. In an asset deal, the purchase price allocation among machinery, inventory, customer intangibles, and goodwill affects both sides' tax bills. Sellers often prefer more goodwill because it typically generates capital gain; buyers prefer allocations to shorter‑lived assets to accelerate deductions. Noncompete and consulting allocations are ordinary income to sellers but amortizable intangibles to buyers, so the economics need to balance. In a stock deal with a 338(h)(10), these same allocation principles apply in the deemed asset sale schedule you file. Early agreement on a realistic allocation tied to an appraisal reduces audit friction later.


Working capital mechanics are another local staple. Seasonal businesses in the Illinois Valley-think construction adjacent trades, distribution tied to agriculture, or hospitality-benefit from a clear definition of normalized working capital and a true‑up that aligns with actual turnover.

Legal updates matter around the edges. Illinois has tightened rules on noncompete agreements for employees under certain income thresholds but continues to allow reasonable noncompetes in the context of selling a business. The state's franchise tax and paid‑in capital filings have been in flux over the past few years; confirm current Secretary of State requirements when planning reorganizations before closing. And while UCC bulk sales law is gone, the Department of Revenue's bulk sale process is alive and well-failing to use it can expose a buyer to the seller's old tax debts.

  • Decide early whether the business realities push you toward asset or stock. Inventory the licenses, contracts with anti‑assignment clauses, and financing preferences of your lender.




  • Model after‑tax proceeds and cash flows under at least three structures: asset sale, stock sale, and stock sale with a step‑up election (or a 754 election for LLCs). Include Illinois sourcing/apportionment.




  • Map regulatory steps and timing.
